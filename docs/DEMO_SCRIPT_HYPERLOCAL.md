## 3:15 — Load the demo problem (why this example is perfect)

**[Click: **Try example problem** — let fields populate]**

> I’m loading a problem that’s *deliberately* unfair to a student in rural Rajasthan.  
>  
> **[Read slowly from the textarea, or paraphrase:]**  
> *“A ski resort in Norway installs large reflective panels to direct sunlight toward icy pathways… Sunlight strikes a panel at **42 degrees** to the normal… find the angle of reflection.”*  
>  
> Count the barriers: **Norway**. **Ski resort**. **Icy pathways**. **Reflective panels** in a climate they may never have seen.  
>  
> The physics is Class 11 — **laws of reflection**.  
> The angle **42°** is the entire mathematical object.  
>  
> Notice the form: subject **Physics**, concept **reflection**, Class **11**.  
> That metadata steers the model so it rewrites *culture*, not *equations*.

## 3:55 — Submit (bridge into the wait)

**[Click: **Localise this ↗** — loader appears]**

> I’ll hit **Localise**.  
>  
> Behind this loader, Groq runs a culturally grounded rewrite, then Noor runs a **mathematical invariance check** — every number in the original must survive.  
> **42 degrees** in; **42 degrees** out.  
> If the model swaps angles or distances, we **retry** — we don’t ship broken maths to a child.

**[Optional voice-over while waiting — don’t rush:]**

> While it runs: imagine this student in Barmer or Jodhpur.  
> They’ve never stood on a Norwegian ski slope — but they *have* seen sunlight hit a **flat mirror** on a rooftop, or a **polished marble** wall at school.  
> That’s the swap we’re asking for: familiar surface, familiar sky, **identical geometry**.
